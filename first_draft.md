
# John

## Meta

### Core Premise 

John is an ordinary man who believes himself capable of something extraordinary. He tells himself he is a writer, though he has never written anything of worth. His life is comfortable, his marriage kind, his work adequate. It is not unhappiness that drives him, but the quiet horror of being unremarkable. Convinced that great art demands great experience, he decides to live — not for living’s sake, but to gather material, as if feeling deeply were a task to be completed. Each new adventure, each risk, each encounter is weighed and measured for what it might offer his unwritten novel. But the more he seeks inspiration, the less he feels. Experience becomes performance; life turns into a thing observed rather than lived. His wife, Gillian, remains his tether to the real world — patient, forgiving, and quietly aware of what he is losing. As John’s search grows desperate, his moral compass dissolves. He mistakes intensity for meaning, confusion for depth. The life he set out to claim becomes a hollow record of his own absence. In the end, the tragedy is not that he fails to write his novel, but that he destroys the man who might have written it. 

### Thematic Brief 

This is a story about a man who confuses *living* with *observing himself live*. John believes he was meant for something greater — that he is, somehow, a writer waiting to be realised. Yet what drives him is not talent, or even curiosity, but the terror of being *ordinary*. His moral weakness lies not in cruelty but in cowardice: he wants to be good, but only so others will see him that way. 

### Authenticity and the performance of life

John’s central tragedy is that he seeks authenticity as a performance. Every act of “experience” — travel, pleasure, even love — becomes material for the novel he intends to write. He doesn’t live for its own sake, but as rehearsal. Each moment is observed, dissected, and weighed for artistic value until no life remains within it. The more he tries to feel, the less he can. 

### Mediocrity, shame, and the longing for greatness

The novel explores the quiet dread of mediocrity — the feeling of being unexceptional in a world that worships success and self-definition. John’s need for significance grows from shame, not ambition. It is the shame of being “normal,” of being unseen. This shame drives him to destroy the very things that give his life quiet meaning. 

### Moral self-deception and the hunger to be good

John’s morality is not absent, but weak. He is full of self-justifications: he never lies outright, only omits; never harms directly, only fails to prevent harm. His self-perception as “a good man” blinds him to the harm he causes. The novel asks what happens when goodness becomes a kind of performance — when decency itself is a role we play for approval. 

### Love as the unrecognised truth

Gillian, his wife, embodies the moral counterpoint. Her love is patient, unadorned, and entirely real. She is the quiet grace that John cannot recognise because it does not announce itself. Through her, the novel suggests that meaning might lie not in intensity or creation, but in ordinary kindness and constancy — things that do not demand to be noticed. 

### The collapse of self through self-awareness

As John’s pursuit of “inspiration” grows more desperate, he turns his gaze inward until nothing is left but the gaze itself. He becomes a man hollowed by reflection — aware of his failure, unable to stop enacting it. The novel closes in on this paradox: that too much consciousness of living can make true life impossible. 

### Art as both salvation and ruin

At its heart, novel asks whether art can redeem an unfulfilled existence, or whether it becomes just another means of avoiding it. John’s failure to write is not a lack of discipline, but of sincerity. In trying to create meaning without having lived it, he annihilates both art and self. His story ends not with revelation, but with quiet ruin — a man who mistook the idea of life for life itself.

## Novel

### Chapter 1

>What is this life if, full of care,  
> We have no time to stand and stare.
- _W. H. Davies

John sat with is back to the door, the open page a white abyss into which he felt his soul falling, much like a hair circling the plug hole of the bath, twirling and drawing with each moment to darkness and oblivion. John was a writer, well thought of himself as one. In the lonely locker of his soul he believed he should be one. His own self was drawn to the idea of being a great... something. And being a novelist is what he had decided he should become.

His study, lit by the glow of the sodium yellow street lamp through the damask curtains behind him was dimming in the twilight of the warm summer's evening. The dust floated in the air, suspended in the state between profound and a nothing of flotsam. The light made the impatient pure glow of his screen sinister, reflecting John thought, the state of his life. Outwardly perfect but inwardly empty and profoundly, inexcusably normal.

"John my love, are you coming down? We promised we would meet Alex and Joshua at eight"

The sound of Gillian drifted up the stairs from living room. Its sound bright, like a child's cry of laughter at a funeral.

"Yes one minute"
"Come on - we don't want to be late again, you can work on your laptop anytime - and Alex and Jo won't be in town again for ages"
"Coming" John replied closing his laptop with a tentative snap.

The light from the study drifted as smoke across the landing and down the stairs
